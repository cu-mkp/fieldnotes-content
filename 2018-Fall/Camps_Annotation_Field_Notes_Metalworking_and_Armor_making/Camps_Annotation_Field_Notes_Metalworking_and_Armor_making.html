Metal

In this first session of the workshop we will be working with 16 gage mild steel.

The gage (also spelled gauge) indicates the thickness of the steel. The higher the number, the thinner the metal; the lower the number, the thicker the metal (Image 3 & 4).

The thickness of the metal used for armor depends on what part of the body you’re trying to protect.

The thicker the metal, the higher the risk it will collapse.

The gage of the metal will change through its reworking (e.g. through the hammering, dishing, etc.).

The quality of steel can be checked through a ‘spark test’. By holding the metal you want to test against a belt sanding machine, sparks will fly off of the metal and turn into stardust. The amount of sparks tells you something about the amount of carbon in the steel.

Not all medieval steel has enough carbon in it for it to be hardened and tempered.

Safety tip: always make sure that the sparks are flying in the direction of the floor (Image 5).

Also pay close attention to the tools you’re using: they are often marked with an arrow indicating which direction to work in.

Subject: Introduction - Measuring the body

The first step in making armor is measuring the body of the wearer (length, circumference, etc.)

Jeff Wasson tries to note down as much information as possible about the body of the wearer.

Leg armor is one of the most important parts of a knight’s armor as this allows the wearer to move around. Jeff always makes a plaster cast of the wearer’s leg to make sure the greaves (and other armor protecting the legs) fit properly (image 6).

Some armor makers also make a full body cast.

According to Jeff Wasson making armor is all about anatomical knowledge; understanding the human body and how it moves.

As Tobias Capwell, curator of arms and armor at the Wallace Collection in London, put it (see minute 32:20 in the link): “[Armor is] engineered to follow the movements of the human body very precisely. Lumbering around, clunking around would be a danger to your knightly dignity as well as to your life.”

Subject: Introduction - Cutting the steel

Material: 16 gage mild steel
Tools:

  • Steel cutting shear

  • Sledge hammer

  • Anvil

When you have measured everything, you need to cut the steel plates into the appropriate shape using a steel shear (image 7).

In the medieval and early modern period, they would not have had such a mechanical shear, but probably used a shear as shown in image (image 8)

Jeff Wasson uses patterns for this so he won’t have to reinvent the wheel every time (image 9).

Although we cannot know for sure, Jeff thinks it is very likely that medieval and early modern armor makers used patterns as well.

To get the right shape, Jeff draws the shape that oughts to be cut with a black marker on the metal sheet (either by freehanding or by tracing the contours of a pattern).

First cut the piece free from the large metal plate, then cut off all of the excess and finally trim it by following the black lines.

To cut, place the sheet in the shear and make sure that the ‘knife’ of the tool will come down on the drawn line (when pulling down the lever). When cutting the steel, it is very important that you don’t go off this drawn line (image 10).

The cutting of the steel may cause for the metal to curve slightly (image 11)].

You therefore need to smoothen this out by placing the piece of metal on an anvil and hammering out the uneven parts (image 12 & 14). Use a hammer with weight and hit it on the curved edges. Always make sure your fingers are removed from the parts to be hit with the hammer (you can also use a clamp/vice grips (image 13) to place the steel on the anvil).

Subject: Introduction - punching holes

Material: steel plate

Tools:

  • Modern technique: steel hole puncher machine

  • Medieval technique:

    • center punch;

    • anvil/woodblock

    • sledge hammer

In order to attach separate pieces of armor to one another, to make armor articulate, or create air holes, you need to punch holes into the metal.

This can be done in two ways:

Modern technique: use a steel hole puncher (image 15) to cut through the metal. This requires placing the sheet of metal in between the puncher and pulling down the lever.

Historical technique:

In the medieval period, they did not have such a machine and they would have done this manually by using a center punch (image 16) and a hammer. The center punch is a metal rod that has a pointy tip and a flat end.

To punch a hole in the metal, you first need to place your sheet on an anvil or woodblock. Then place the center punch on the place where you want to create the hole. Keep the center punch in place while striking on its flat end with a hammer (image 17).

This will first create a divot (a disruption in the surface level). Then use a flat punch (i.e. with a flat tip) to make this divot more pronounced (sometimes you need to hammer several times on the disrupted metal surface to punch a hole in the material).

When you’re working with thick metal it might be difficult to punch through the metal. Heating the metal first will soften the material, thus making it easier to punch a hole.

Difference modern and historical technique:

In addition to the fact that using a cutting machine is faster than punching a hole manually, there is another difference between the modern and historical technique.

As can be seen in image 18, in contrast to the modern cutting machine, the center punch leaves a trace in the form of a bur; that is, the surface level contours of the hole are not smooth or even, but are slightly raised. This would have had to be evened out by means of sanding/grinding.

Subject: Introduction - Deburring and beveling edges

Material: steel plate

Tools: belt sanding machine, angle grinder

Safety: safety goggles

Similar to the process of preparing a printing plate (see entry ‘2018.10.22, 13:10 pm’ in link), the edges of steel pieces for armor also need to be deburred.

In the context of printing this is needed so the sharp edges won’t damage your printing paper.

In the context of armor making deburring the edges of metal serves a somewhat similar purpose: Since armor serves a functional purpose (i.e. in that it will be worn and used) it is crucial to bevel/debur the edges of the steel plates. Knights wore jackets/coats underneath their armor and sharp edges could damage these or, worse, injure the wearer. Additionally, swords or other weapons could get easily caught in sharp or rough edges. Beveled edges could prevent this: swords striking the armor would slide right off of beveled edges.

Jeff Wasson uses an angle grinder and/or belt sanding machine (Image 19) to bevel the edges of the steel.

In the middle ages (or early modern period), they would most likely have used a file. Beveling and sanding by hand is time consuming. Given that we only have a limited amount of time, we will bevel and sand mechanically.

Always hold the steel at the edges and be careful that your fingers and knuckles are not too close to the grinder/sanding machine. The belt rotates at high speed and you can injure yourself severely if too close to the machine.

Never sand into the edge (Image 20). The rotating belt will always want to bounce off the metal if you do so. Always sand off of the edges (Image 21).

To sand, move your steel plate from left to right alongside the rotating belt. Keep enough pressure, but not too much as this will remove too much metal.

Subject: Introduction - Dishing

Material: Steel plate

Tools:

  • Sledge hammer

  • Wood block/anvil

  • Vice grips

Safety: Safety goggles

Dishing (also called sinking) is the next step in the process. It is a pretty modern technique, but according to Jeff Wasson they might have also known about this in the Middle Ages.

Dishing involves both giving shape and volume to the metal and to strengthen it (through hammering).

Hammering will harden the metal, which will make it stronger and increase its resistance to weapons (and thus improve its protecting abilities).

Yet making the metal too hard (hammering too much) will make it brittle and more likely to crack.

Armor can have different shapes

  • round (e.g. a helmet) --

  • gutter shaped (like a tube)

  • anticlastic (e.g. greaves)
    This shape is easier to achieve when the metal is hot.

Hammers can have different shapes and weights (image 22)

Always use a sledge hammer with mass (i.e. weight). Always hammer on an anvil or wood block.

In order to shape, or to curve, the metal place your sheet on a woodblock with hollows in it (image 23) and push the metal into the hole by hitting it with the hammer (image 24). Make sure that the sheet is making contact around the hollows. You can use vice grips to hold the metal, place it on the woodblock and prevent hammering on your fingers.

If you hit the metal in the right place, it will bent into the hollow of the wood and take on its shape.

For deep hollows, use a hammer with a round face.

In order to shape or curve the metal the way you want it to, you need to hit the metal on the right spot. This requires having control over the hammer, and in turn, over your wrist.

If you don’t have control over either of the two, the hammer will hit the metal all over the place and it will be bent out of shape.

N.B. to get an even curve, you need to exert the right amount of pressure with the hammer on all sides.

If your sheet is lumpy/bumpy, hammer it more to smoothen it.

Experience:

I struggled striking the metal at the same and right spot and my hammer bounced off the metal repeatedly. This indicated I wasn’t in good control of my hammer and I thus needed to have a firmer grip on the tool. My wrist action was too loose. Firming that grip also made me experience how tiring hammering can be on your body (having to keep a firm grip and exerting a sufficient amount of pressure on the metal over an extended period of time), especially when making large pieces of armor.

Subject: Introduction - Planishing

Materials: Steel plate

Tools:

  • Sledge hammer

  • Anvil

  • Vice grips

Safety: safety goggles

Planishing is the hammering of the metal to even out the irregularities created by the dishing. In doing so, you will equally add more volume to the metal sheet.

Planishing basically means squeezing the metal sheet between an anvil and a hammer.

Key to planishing is finding the right hammer; that is, a hammer that matches the shape of the the metal sheet you’re working with and has the right weight.

If you choose a hammer that is too light, you won’t have enough impact with each blow and you will end up planishing for a long time.

You can planish on either the inside or the outside of the metal.

When making a helmet, for example, it will be difficult to find a hammer that matches the strong curve of the helmet. In this case, it is best to planish on the outside of the metal by placing it on a ball stake (image 27 & image 28) and use a hammer with a flat face. Make sure to always hit the center of the helmet (which is in contact with the ball stake). The edges of the hammer are sharp and hitting the metal at the wrong angle could cause dents.

As with the dishing, you want to make sure that the face of the hammer has the best possible impact with each blow. In order to do this you need to hit the metal on the same spot repeatedly and with the same impact in order to get an even curve and to evenly smoothen out the irregularities.

To have more control over the blow of the hammer and to make sure you hit the metal at the same spot, hold the hammer closer to its head (with a firm wrist grip). If your wrist movement is too loose, the hammer will hit the metal in various places and you won’t get an even curve.

Sound (i.e. your senses) is very important in this part of the process. What you want to hear is a solid ring when striking the metal with the hammer. When you hear a hollow sound upon striking the metal, you know that the spot you are targeting is not in full contact with the surface of the anvil and you’re holding your sheet at the wrong angle. This can be corrected, either by hitting the metal at a different spot --which is touching the anvil-- or by repositioning the metal, i.e. changing the angle at which you’re holding it. This is where the vice grips come in handy. You can move and turn the metal easily while hammering (without running the risk of hitting your fingers or letting the metal sheet slip from your hands).

It will take practice to find the right amount of pressure to exert with the hammer.

When done correctly, the planishing should leave small circles across the surface level of the metal (image 29).

According to Jeff Wasson, the tools that one uses in the making of armor leave their traces/marks.

Subject: Introduction - Polishing

Material: Steel plate

Tools: various grinding machines (see below)

Safety: safety glasses, ear protection

The next step in the process is polishing the armor to give it a shiny finish.

Polishing will also remove tool marks left on the metal in the process of dishing, planishing, hole cutting etc.

General tips for polishing:

Direction:

Jeff advises to polish your plate in the same direction. That is, moving your plate against the grinding machines from left to right and not from left to right and back (i.e. cross direction).

However, if you really want that high mirror finish, you need to polish in cross direction.

Consistency:

For all polishing the rule is to be consistent. If you are making a full armor you want each separate piece to have the same type of finish. That is, you don’t want to have the breast plate and greaves showing a rough finish and have the shoulder defense mirror polished.

Grit:

As with regular sanding paper, belts come in different grits as well.

The number of the grit tells you something about the particle size.

The smaller the number, the coarser the grit; the higher the number, the finer the grit.

Modern Machines:

Jeff Wasson uses various machines for polishing.

  1. Belt sander:

  • The belt sander is used for general beveling, deburring and sanding.

  • Jeff has two types of grits for the belt: 60 and 80. We are currently using a 60 grit belt.

    • Compare this, for example, to the grit of the sandpaper used in the sanding of a copper plate for printing and the one used in our preparation of the metal sheets for amor-- see entry ‘2018.10.22, 10:05 pm’ in the link). For the copper plate we used a fine grit of 600 and 1200. For the armor sheets we are using a more coarse grit between 80 and 180.

2. The second machine (image 33) also has a rotating sandpaper belt, but of a finer grit: 80.

  • This machine has a horizontal rotating belt (as opposed to machine number 1, whose belt rotates vertically). This allows you to sand or polish metal sheets shaped or curved in a certain way better than you could with belt sander 1.

  • Using this machine will refine the polishing

3. Flap sander wheel:

  • This wheel is made of a series of pieces of sandpaper on a wheel (image 34 & 35).

  • Jeff uses a grit of 120 and 180

  • When used after sanding machines 1 & 2, the flap sander machine will give your sheet a highly shiny look (approximating the mirror finish).

Historical technique:

* They probably used a file to polish armor and perhaps a mill or water driven wheel. According to Jeff, the polishing by hand with a file would only take away “mountains”. The flap sander wheel on the other hand, will go into the “hills and valleys”. It will allow you to create a uniform curvature instead of having your sheet look modeled and uneven.

Both Jonathan Tavares and Jeff Wasson have pointed out that historically speaking polishing was very time consuming and expensive. One third of the cost of armor lay in its polishing.

As a consequence, one had to really trust polishers to do their job well.

Historical and modern aesthetics

Historical armor displays much more marks/scratches than modern-made armor.

According to Jeff Wasson we are so concerned nowadays with symmetry and perfection that we tend to forget this was not the concern of armor makers in the medieval period. These practitioners were much more interested and concerned with shape and an organic aesthetic.

Modern armor is also often characterized by a highly shiny, mirror finish, which is indicative of our aesthetic preference.

With historical armor, in contrast, you see a lot more different types and levels of finish, ranging from rough to a highly polished mirror finish.

Question:

To what extent can the level and type of finish of historical armor tell us something about the skills of a polisher? Additionally, if polishing was expensive and a high amount of polishing would be more costly than less polishing, can the finish of period armor then also tell us more about the status and economic position of the wearer/commissioner (I am assuming that someone who was well endowed could probably afford more polishing to be done on his armor than someone of lower social standing)?

Answer Jeff Wasson: Yes. Both.

Dukes would often wear highly polished and gilded armor whereas the armor of a common soldier would probably have a rough(er) finish.

But achieving a mirror finish would also require skill. Not all polishers were able to do this.

Subject: Introduction - Making a crest

Material: Steel plate

Tools:

  • Shear/steel cutter

  • Anvil

  • Woodblock

  • Hammers

  • Sanding machines

  • Stakes

  • Chisels

  • Bench-screw

Safety:

Crests are basically corrugations or folds in the metal and were added to various pieces of armor including (but not limited to):

  • Besagues (image 39): besagues, like rondels (circular in shape), could hang off the breastplate and served to protect the armpits. Before the 15th century, armpits were not protected by a long shoulder defense (pauldrons) and were thus vulnerable (soldiers wore mail underneath their armor for this reason). From the 15th century you see that shoulder defense in armor is going all the way up the neck to fill this gap at the armpit. The downside of this type of shoulder defense was that they limited a soldier’s/knight’s ability to move and added to the weight of an armor.

N.B. According to Jeff, armor is all about the balance between mobility, protection and

aesthetics.

Question:

What is the purpose of crests? Are they purely aesthetic or do they serve a practical purpose as well?

Answer Jeff Wasson: the crest served various purposes:

  • Decoration (German gothic armor, for example, is known for its crests and flutes)

  • Adding rigidity to the piece of armor

  • Protective: a crested piece of armor could act to either stop, for example, the strike of a sword, or glance it off, thus preventing a weapon from cutting straight through the metal.

Tempering and quenching

Tempering is the modern process of heat treating the metal in order to harden it, making it tougher.

You can use a blow torch for this (image 108). It is important to see the color of the metal when you are tempering or heat treating it, so you will have to clean your metal piece before you start.

When the metal reaches a certain temperature (but has not yet reached its critical point where it becomes incandescent), it will become magnetic (image 109). This is one way to check the temperature of the metal without having to use a thermometer.

When it becomes non-magnetic, it has reached its critical temperature (just before it will turn red-hot), which is when you need to quench the metal in oil or, as Jeff does, in a mixture of water and polymer.

It is important to cool off the metal slowly. If you cool it off too fast, the crystals in the metal will form in such a way that will toughen the metal, but will also make it brittle. Water is too aggressive.

Oil or the water-polymer mixture is better as this allows the metal to cool off slowly.

Jeff uses a water-polymer mixture instead of oil -- as they would have done in the middle ages -- as this doesn’t splash (as oil does).

Although, ideally, you want hard, tough armor, you don’t want it to go beyond that crucial point where it gets so hard that it gets brittle. It is therefore better to have metal be a bit more dense or softer, than to have it crack because you hardened it too much.

Tempering and the senses

One way of testing the toughness/hardness of metal is by means of a sound test. This is what they might have done in the middle ages.

They would use a file and rub it over the metal to listen to the kind of sound it produced. Hard, tempered metal sounds differently from non-tempered metal.

Darker: 750 degrees

Black: 800 degrees

Red-hot (incandescent): 900 degrees

Colored metal can partly prevent rust and can last for years if you keep it in a dry place (and don’t let it get wet). You can protect the colored metal by coating it with wax or oil.

If you polish a colored piece of metal, you will rub of its color. Polishing colored armor is thus not possible -- at least not if you want it to retain its color.

The forge is too hot to color the metal. Jeff therefore uses a small blow torch (image 110).

By moving the flame of the torch back and forth as well as sideways under and over the piece of metal, you can change the material’s color (image 111; 112; 113).

Once the metal has reached the desired color, you need to quench it, to prevent it from passing beyond that point.

In the middle ages they probably used a forge or other heat source and then a second ‘chamber’ or ‘room’ or something like that to redirect the heat of the forge to. They could then use this second ‘chamber’/ ‘room’ to heat treat and color their metal.

Flatten the end of the rod

What we’re aiming to do is flatten (i.e. stretch) the end of the rod (image 130). You can do this by pushing the metal outwards by hitting it with a hammer when it is hot.

It is best to practice the hammering on the cold metal first, to become familiar with the technique of knowing where to hit the hot metal. In this way, you can be as efficient as possible when the metal comes out of the forge, and you won’t lose any valuable time.

To push the metal outward evenly at all sides, it is best to use the round side of the hammer.

When you think you’re ready, have organized your tools and have practiced the hammering enough, turn on the forge and place the end of the long steel rod/bar in the forge (image 131).
